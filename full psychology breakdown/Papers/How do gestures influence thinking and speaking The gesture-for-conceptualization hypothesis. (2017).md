# How do gestures influence thinking and speaking? The gesture-for-conceptualization hypothesis.

**Authors:** Sotaro Kita, Mingyuan Chu
**Year:** 2017
**Citations:** 329
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1037/rev0000059

## Abstract

People spontaneously produce gestures during speaking and thinking. The authors focus here on gestures that depict or indicate information related to the contents of concurrent speech or thought (i.e., representational gestures). Previous research indicates that such gestures have not only communicative functions, but also self-oriented cognitive functions. In this article, the authors propose a new theoretical framework, the gesture-for-conceptualization hypothesis, which explains the self-oriented functions of representational gestures.
