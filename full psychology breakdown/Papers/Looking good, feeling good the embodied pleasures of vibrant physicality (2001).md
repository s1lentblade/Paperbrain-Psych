## Abstract

Social scientists of medicine have largely, although by no means exclusively, focused their research on illness and sickness thus obscuring social scientific investigations of positive health and wellbeing. Undoubtedly, important reasons exist for this but the relevance of studying ‘healthy’ bodies requires emphasis and wider acknowledgement within the newer (embodied, non‐dualistic) sociology of health and illness. This is necessary because the concrete corporeal manifestations of ‘health’ in everyday life – components of and preconditions for embodied social practice – may, paradoxically, erode bodily capital while simultaneously contributing to it.
