# Relation of threatened egotism to violence and aggression: The dark side of high self-esteem.

**Year:** 1996
**Citations:** 2,300
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295x.103.1.5

## Abstract

Conventional wisdom has regarded low self-esteem as an important cause of violence, but the opposite view is theoretically viable. An interdisciplinary review of evidence about aggression, crime, and violence contradicted the view that low self-esteem is an important cause. Instead, violence appears to be most commonly a result of threatened egotism--that is, highly favorable views of self that are disputed by some person or circumstance. Inflated, unstable, or tentative beliefs in the self's superiority may be most prone to encountering threats and hence to causing violence.
